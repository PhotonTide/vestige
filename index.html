Vestige  ·  A collection by Photon Tide
On origin

Before art there was music. At fifteen I was writing poetry over instrumentals, teaching myself keyboard and guitar in the same breath — trying to find a language for things I didn't yet have words for. Music gave me that. It still does. There's a particular state it puts me in: focused and unraveling at the same time, everything deliberate and nothing controlled. Flow, I think, is the closest word.

Music is also what led me to visual art. That impulse to translate one thing into another never left me. I eventually stepped back from releasing music to commit fully to visual art, but the two have always been the same conversation. Vestige is where they finally meet.

On the concept

Sound is the most ephemeral of art forms. It exists in time, completely, and then it's gone. A painting stays. A photograph stays. Music asks you to hold it in memory, and memory distorts.

A vestige is what remains after something has passed — a trace, a remnant, proof that something was here. Each piece in this collection is exactly that: the visual residue of a piece of music. Not an illustration of it. Not a representation. The actual frequencies, the energy, the movement of the sound — translated in real time into line and color and mark, and then made permanent.

The sound ends. This stays.

On the process

Upload any audio. The system reads the music sixty times per second, decomposing it into its raw frequencies. Bass frequencies build the sweeping arcs of color at the core of each piece. Mid-range frequencies — where melody and harmony live — trace the fine flowing lines that cover the surface like breath or hair. High frequencies leave stippled marks. Sudden changes in the spectrum, the moments where one thing becomes another, leave their own traces.

The palette is derived from the audio itself: a hash of the waveform generates a unique two-color combination for each track, drawn from a curated range of muted, analogous tones. The same song will always produce the same painting. A different song — even slightly different — produces something entirely its own.

You choose when to mint. Beginning, middle, end — each is a different piece. Early in a song the canvas is sparse, tentative. Later it's dense with everything that happened. The painting is a record of how far in you were willing to go.
